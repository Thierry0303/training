What happens when you tap?

You tap your card and the screen says "Approved" in under two seconds. But in those two seconds, your card information has crossed the globe.

It starts with a question

When your card touches the reader, the payment terminal sends a message to your bank: "This person wants to spend £49.99. Do you allow it?"

The message travels through four places

That question doesn't go straight to your bank. It travels: Your tap → Shop's bank → Visa/Mastercard network → Your bank.

Your bank checks your account, says yes or no, and the answer travels back.

Key point: Visa and Mastercard don't hold your money. They're like telephone exchanges connecting your bank to the shop's bank.

Four people in every payment

Every card payment has four different parties working together: the cardholder (you), the merchant (shop), the acquirer (shop's bank), and the issuer (your bank).

Who they are

Cardholder: You, the person paying

Merchant: The business being paid (the coffee shop)

Acquirer: The merchant's bank (Stripe, Barclays)

Issuer: Your bank (Monzo, HSBC, Chase)

Why four? Each bank needs to verify independently. The issuer checks you have the money. The acquirer checks the transaction is real. Visa connects them and makes sure everyone trusts each other.

Does money move instantly?

Your card is approved instantly. But money? That moves 1-2 days later in a batch.

Approval vs Settlement

When you tap, your bank puts a hold on your money. This is approval. But the actual money movement (settlement) happens later.

The timeline

Moment of tap: Card approved instantly

End of day: Shop sends all its transactions to its bank

Overnight: Banks calculate who owes whom

Next 1-2 days: Money actually moves

Why wait? Processing trillions of payments one-by-one would be slow and expensive. Batching is efficient.

What those 16 numbers mean

Your card number isn't random. Every digit tells the payment system something important.

Breaking it down

Take: 4532 0151 1283 0366

1st digit (4): The network. "4" = Visa. "5" = Mastercard.

1st–6th digits: Your bank code. Tells the network which bank issued it.

7th–15th: Your account number inside that bank.

16th: A check digit that catches typos.

Why structure matters: The first 6 digits are crucial. The terminal reads these first to know which bank to contact.

Chip vs swipe vs tap

Three ways to use a card. Very different security.

Magnetic stripe (swipe)

The black stripe stores your data. When you swipe, it's read. Problem: same data every time. A criminal copies it once = unlimited use.

Chip (insert)

The gold chip is a tiny computer. Every tap generates a unique secret code for that one transaction only. Even if captured, it's useless. Chip cloning is nearly impossible.

Contactless (tap)

Radio waves instead of physical contact. Same unique-per-transaction code as chip. Capped at £100 because above that, you need to prove it's you with a PIN.

Why cards get declined

It's almost never the shop's fault. Your bank said no.

Your bank decides

When declined, your bank said no. The shop can't see why — they just get a code.

Common reasons

Not enough funds: Balance too low

Card expired: Date passed

Bank suspects fraud: Unusual transaction

Daily limit: Already spent limit

Wrong PIN: Too many failed tries

What to do: Check your banking app. Try another payment method. Call the number on your card if unsure.
