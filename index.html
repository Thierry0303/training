Module 1 · Chapter 1 · Lesson 1

What happens when you tap?

When you tap your card, it crosses the globe and comes back in 2 seconds. Here's the complete journey.

It starts with a question

The moment your card touches the reader, the terminal asks your bank: "This person wants to spend £49.99. Do you allow it?"

The message travels through four places

Your tap → Shop's bank → Visa/Mastercard network → Your bank → Back again.

Key point: Visa and Mastercard don't hold your money. They route the question — your bank gives the answer.

Module 1 · Chapter 1 · Lesson 2

Four people in every payment

Every card payment has four parties: you, the merchant, the acquirer, and the issuer.

Who they are

You (cardholder): The person paying

Merchant: The business (shop, airline, website)

Acquirer: Merchant's bank (Stripe, Barclays)

Issuer: Your bank (Monzo, HSBC, Chase)

Why four? Each bank verifies independently. The network connects everyone and ensures trust.

Module 1 · Chapter 1 · Lesson 3

Does money move instantly?

Approval is instant. Money moving is 1–2 days later.

Approval vs Settlement

Approval: your bank puts a hold on your money. Settlement: the actual money movement (hours or days later).

The timeline

Tap: Approved instantly. End of day: Shop batches transactions. Next 1–2 days: Money actually moves.

Why wait? Batching trillions of payments is efficient. Processing them one-by-one would be slow.

Module 1 · Chapter 2 · Lesson 4

What those 16 numbers mean

Every digit tells the network something essential.

Breaking it down

Card: 4532 0151 1283 0366

1st digit (4): Network. 4 = Visa, 5 = Mastercard.

1st–6th digits: Bank code. Which bank issued it.

7th–15th: Your account number.

16th: Check digit. Catches typos.

Why structure? First 6 digits are the bank identifier. Terminal uses these to route correctly.

Module 1 · Chapter 2 · Lesson 5

Chip vs swipe vs tap

Three ways to pay. Very different security.

Magnetic stripe (swipe)

Same data every time. Copy it once = unlimited use. Security: low.

Chip (insert)

Generates unique code per transaction. Captured code is useless. Security: very high.

Contactless (tap)

Radio waves, unique code per tap. Capped at £100 — above that, need PIN. Security: very high.

BUnique one-time code
CFingerprint encrypted
DNo data stored
Module 1 · Chapter 2 · Lesson 6

Why cards get declined

Almost never the shop's fault. Your bank said no.

Common reasons

Not enough funds: Balance too low. Card expired: Date passed. Fraud flag: Unusual activity. Daily limit: Already spent max. Wrong PIN: Too many tries.

What to do: Check your app. Try another card. Call your bank if unsure.

Module 1 · Chapter 3 · Lesson 7

Online payments need extra protection

In-store you prove it's you with your PIN. Online, only the number is needed.

The problem

Anyone with your card number, expiry, and CVV can buy online pretending to be you. This is why online fraud is 10× more common than in-store.

The solution: 3-D Secure

When you shop online, the website asks your bank: "Is this really your customer?" Your bank then asks you to prove it via text code or app.

In Europe this is law: PSD2 regulations require Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) for online payments. This is why you see verification steps.

Module 1 · Chapter 3 · Lesson 8

That verification text explained

The OTP (one-time password) proves you're the real cardholder.

What it does

Your bank texts you a code. You enter it. This proves you have your phone — and therefore are likely the real cardholder, not a fraudster.

Why you don't always get one

Your bank scores each transaction for risk. Low-risk ones (familiar site, familiar amount, familiar device) are approved "frictionlessly" — no interruption. High-risk ones require the code.

Critical: Never share your OTP with anyone. Your real bank will never ask for it by phone.

DAsk for email first
Module 1 · Chapter 4 · Lesson 9

How card fraud actually happens

Understanding how fraudsters operate is the best defence.

Main methods

Phishing: Fake email with link to fake site. Data breach: Hacked website leaks your card. ATM skimming: Hidden reader + camera captures PIN. Social engineering: Phone call convinces you to share details.

Red flags on a call: "Account will be closed today." "Move money to safe account." "Give me your PIN." "Download this app." Real banks never do these.

Module 1 · Chapter 4 · Lesson 10

Chargebacks — getting money back

Your right to dispute a payment and get refunded.

What is a chargeback?

Your bank forcibly reverses a payment on your behalf. You dispute it, your bank investigates, and if legitimate, they take the money back from the merchant and return it to you.

When you can use it

Goods never arrived. Goods different from description. Charged twice. Subscription charged after cancellation. Fraud. Business closed.

Section 75 (UK): On credit cards for purchases £100–£30,000, your card company is equally liable with the merchant. Even stronger than chargeback.

DCall Visa directly
Module 1 · Chapter 5 · Lesson 11

What accepting cards costs shops

Merchants don't keep 100% of your payment. A slice goes to the bank and networks.

The fee breakdown

You pay £100. Merchant receives ~£98–£99 (depending on card type). The difference is split: your bank (interchange, funds their cashback), Visa/Mastercard (network fee), merchant's bank (acquiring fee).

Premium cards cost more: A travel rewards card has higher fees than a basic debit card. Merchants effectively subsidise your air miles.
Quick Check

DAirline keeps full amount
Module 1 · Chapter 5 · Lesson 12

When shops actually get paid

You tap Saturday. Shop thinks they're paid. Money arrives Monday–Tuesday.

The gap

Approval is instant. Payment (settlement) is 1–2 working days later. Shop must plan cash flow around this.

Why the wait?

End of day: shop batches all transactions. Overnight: banks calculate. T+1 or T+2: money actually moves.

New businesses wait longer: Payment processors hold a "reserve" (5–15%) for weeks/months in case of chargebacks. Working capital squeeze is real.

Module 2 · Chapter 6 · Lesson 13

How 3DS decides when to ask for proof

3DS isn't one-size-fits-all. It intelligently scores risk and decides: interrupt the customer or approve silently?

Risk scoring

Your bank's system evaluates dozens of signals in real time: device (is it your usual phone?), location (matches your home?), merchant (shopped here before?), amount (normal for you?), velocity (buying a lot right now?)

Two outcomes: Frictionless (bank confident it's you, no interrupt) or Challenge (bank wants you to verify via OTP or app).

AFrictionless — under £100
BChallenge — unfamiliar country is high-risk

DFrictionless — only domestic transactions
Module 2 · Chapter 6 · Lesson 14

Liability shift — who pays for fraud?

3DS does more than verify. It reassigns who's financially responsible when fraud happens.

Before 3DS

Merchant accepted payment with no verification. Fraud happened. Merchant loses.

After 3DS

Payment was authenticated. Fraud happened. Bank loses (they authenticated someone claiming to be the cardholder).

The business reason merchants use 3DS: They shift fraud liability to the bank. It's risk protection, not just security.
Quick Check
Understanding liability
Merchant skipped 3DS using "low value" exemption. Transaction turns out fraudulent. Who loses?
AMerchant — they skipped it and kept liability
BBank — low value = automatic responsibility
CVisa/Mastercard — they cover small fraud
DCardholder — they should notice sooner
Module 2 · Chapter 6 · Lesson 15

SCA exemptions — when to skip verification

Requiring 3DS for every transaction would be exhausting. So the rules include exemptions.

Main exemptions

Low value: Under €30 per transaction. Trusted merchant: You've told your bank to trust them. Recurring billing: Netflix charges after you authenticated once. Corporate/B2B: Different rules for business cards.

The trade-off: Less friction for you, but merchant retains fraud liability. No 3DS = no liability shift.
Quick Check
Understanding exemptions
Netflix charges you monthly. After initial auth, no more 3DS prompts. Why allowed?
AMIT exemption — merchant initiated after consent
BSCA required every time by law
COnly if amount under €30
DOutside EU only
Module 2 · Chapter 7 · Lesson 16

Why Apple Pay doesn't send your card number

Tokenisation: your real card number stays at your bank. A substitute is used instead.

The problem

Every merchant you shop with sees your card number. Hack one merchant = card stolen. The solution: never send the real number.

The solution: tokens

Your phone gets a Device Primary Account Number (DPAN) — a substitute. Your real number (PAN) stays at the bank. Only the token is transmitted.

Two security layers: (1) Token only works on your device. (2) Each transaction generates a unique cryptogram — captured tokens can't be replayed.
Quick Check
Understanding tokenisation
Retailer's database hacked, tokens stolen. Why less damaging than real card numbers?
ATokens are encrypted
BGovernment insures tokens
CTokens only work on specific device with cryptogram
DTokens expire after 24h
Module 2 · Chapter 7 · Lesson 17

How cards get into digital wallets

Adding a card to Apple Pay looks simple. Behind it: a six-party security handshake.

The provisioning process

1. You photograph card. 2. Apple sends to Token Service Provider. 3. TSP looks up which bank issued it. 4. Your bank is asked to approve. 5. You verify (text code or app). 6. DPAN token is generated and stored securely on your phone.

Each wallet has a TRID: Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Pay each have unique IDs. Banks know which wallet tokens came from.
Quick Check
Understanding provisioning
You add card to Apple Pay and get a verification text. What is your bank approving?
AA payment you're making now
BToken creation and storage on your device
CApple managing your account
DChanges to contactless limit
Module 2 · Chapter 8 · Lesson 18

How banks score every transaction for risk

In milliseconds, your bank scores each payment 0–100 for fraud likelihood.

Key signals

Device: Your usual phone? IP: VPN or residential? Behaviour: Did you paste or type? History: Normal amount/merchant? Velocity: Many transactions suddenly? Merchant: High-fraud category?

Context matters: Score of 40 on £10 pizza = nothing. Score of 40 on £3000 electronics = challenge likely. Amount + context = decision.

Module 2 · Chapter 8 · Lesson 19

Velocity rules — catching fraud patterns

Fast, simple rules: if X happens more than Y times in Z seconds, trigger action.

Examples

Same card 4+ times in 1 hour: Rapid testing. Same IP 3+ cards in 24h: Bot attack. 5+ charges under £1 in 10 min: Card enumeration. Same device 3+ cards in 48h: Account takeover.

False positives happen: Someone buying multiple gifts triggers "same address" rule. Developers testing trigger "tiny charges" rule. Fraud teams manage exceptions.
Quick Check
Understanding velocity

ALarge amount threshold
BMultiple small charges from different cards on same IP
CForeign IP
DWeekend rule

Inside a chargeback — step by step

What happens from dispute to resolution.

The timeline

Day 0: Cardholder contacts bank. Day 1–30: Issuer files chargeback. Day 5–45: Merchant notified, has 20–45 days to respond. Day 75–180: If still disputed, goes to arbitration.

Merchant costs per chargeback: Lost sale amount + goods/service already delivered + processing fees already paid + chargeback fee (£15–£35) + staff time. Real cost: 2–3× the transaction amount.

Module 2 · Chapter 9 · Lesson 21

Reason codes — why disputes get categorised

Every chargeback has a code. Code determines what evidence merchant needs and odds of winning.

Visa codes

10.4: Card-absent fraud (most common). 13.1: Merchandise not received. 13.3: Not as described.

Mastercard codes

4853: Cardholder disputes. 4863: Not recognised. 4870/4871: Liability shift (didn't use chip/PIN).

Allocation vs Collaboration: Fraud codes (10.4) auto-assign liability based on 3DS. Service codes (13.1, 13.3) require merchant to provide evidence.

Module 2 · Chapter 10 · Lesson 22

Interchange economics

Interchange is the largest fee in every transaction. Who sets it? Why?

What determines rates?

Debit vs credit: Credit = higher (0.3–1.5%), debit = lower (0.2%). Card tier: Standard < Gold < Platinum. Card present vs online: Online = higher (more fraud). Merchant type: Supermarkets lower, luxury goods higher. EU cap: 0.2% debit, 0.3% credit (post-Brexit: much higher for cross-border).

Who pays: Shop pays to your bank (via acquirer). Your bank uses its share to fund your cashback, travel insurance, rewards.
Quick Check
Understanding interchange
Restaurant's card fees higher with premium travel cards vs basic debit. Why?
APremium cards need more processing
BPremium cards have higher interchange to fund air miles
CPremium cards are more fraudulent
DPremium holders spend more percentage-wise
Module 2 · Chapter 10 · Lesson 23

PCI DSS — card security standards

Not a law, but enforced by card schemes. Non-compliance = fines, termination.

The 12 requirements (simplified)

1–2: Network & system security. 3–4: Protect and encrypt card data. 5–6: Malware & vulnerability protection. 7–8: Access control & authentication. 9: Physical security. 10–11: Logging & monitoring. 12: Written policies.

Scope reduction: Use hosted payment pages (Stripe, Adyen) and card data never touches your servers. PCI scope drops from 12 requirements to SAQ A (short questionnaire).
Quick Check
Understanding PCI

BMinimal — SAQ A self-assessment
CNone — only banks need PCI
DMust store encrypted card numbers
Module 2 · Chapter 10 · Lesson 24

How merchant risk is assessed

Before a business gets card acceptance, acquirers perform due diligence. Ongoing monitoring continues.

KYB checks

Legal entity & ownership: Verify UBOs (beneficial owners). Business model: What are they selling? Financial health: Can they cover chargebacks? Processing history: Chargeback & fraud rates at previous processors. Website & terms: Clear refund policies?

High-risk categories

Online gambling, adult content, crypto exchanges, pharma, travel (high chargebacks), subscriptions with free trials.

Rolling reserves: New/risky merchants have 5–15% held for 90–180 days. This ties up working capital significantly.

Module 3 · Chapter 11 · Lesson 25

Reading an ISO 8583 message

ISO 8583 is the messaging standard for every card transaction on earth.

Message structure

MTI (Message Type Indicator): Form number. 0100 = auth request, 0110 = response. Bitmap: Checklist of which fields present. Data elements: Actual data (card, amount, auth code).

Key fields

DE 2: Card number. DE 4: Amount. DE 22: Entry mode (chip, tap, swipe). DE 39: Response code (00=approved, 51=insufficient funds). DE 55: EMV chip data with ARQC cryptogram.

Why structure? Every bank uses the same form globally. No ambiguity about what Field 2 means.
Quick Check
Understanding ISO 8583
Message returns MTI 0110, DE 39 = "51". Meaning?
AAuth request sent, approved
BReversal approved
CResponse — declined, insufficient funds
DDebit request sent, approved
Module 3 · Chapter 11 · Lesson 26

SMS vs DMS — two settlement architectures

Single Message vs Dual Message. Very different implications for operations.

DMS (Dual Message) — Credit cards

Two messages: Auth (0100/0110) now, clearing message later. Merchant must "capture" to settle. Hotel pre-authorises £500, adjusts at checkout to £347, captures £347.

SMS (Single Message) — Debit

One message (0200/0210): Auth = settlement. Capture isn't needed. Funds debit in real time.

Operationally

DMS: must capture or customer charged but you get no money. SMS: fire and forget.

Critical difference: Not capturing a DMS auth = you lose money. Monitoring capture success is essential.
Quick Check
Understanding DMS/SMS
Hotel pre-auths £500, bill is £347. Under DMS, what should they do?
ACharge full £500
BCapture £347, £153 hold releases
CRefund £153 after charging £500
DVoid and re-auth for £347
Module 3 · Chapter 12 · Lesson 27

3DS technical flows

Inside the 3DS authentication protocol: five parties, JSON messages, real-time decisions.

The AReq/ARes flow

1. Fingerprinting: JavaScript collects device data silently. 2. AReq: Merchant sends to Directory Server, routed to ACS with full context. 3. ACS decides: Frictionless (Y), challenge (C), or decline (N). 4. ARes: Response to merchant. 5. Challenge (if C): OTP, biometric, or push notification. 6. CAVV: On success, cryptogram proves 3DS was completed.

Frictionless rate depends on data quality: Rich AReq (account age, transaction history, device fingerprint) = higher frictionless. Minimal AReq = more challenges, more abandonment.
Quick Check
Understanding 3DS flows
ACS responds "C". What happens next?
ADeclined (C = cancelled)
BChallenge presented to cardholder
CMerchant calls issuer
DFrictionless approval
Module 3 · Chapter 12 · Lesson 28

ECI values — authentication outcomes

The Electronic Commerce Indicator (ECI) tells the issuer exactly what 3DS happened.

Visa ECI

05: Fully authenticated. 06: Authentication attempted (ACS returned unable). 07: No authentication attempted.

Liability

05 & 06: Shifts to issuer (attempted counts). 07: Stays with merchant.

Attempted = protected: Even if 3DS failed, attempting it shifts liability to issuer. Only ECI 07 (no attempt) means merchant keeps risk.
Quick Check
Understanding ECI
Visa transaction completes ECI 06. Later disputed as fraud. Who bears chargeback?
AMerchant — ECI 06 = failed
BIssuer — attempted = liability shift
CVisa
D50/50
Module 3 · Chapter 13 · Lesson 29

VTS, MDES and Token Service Providers

Network tokenisation run by the card schemes themselves.

Who they are

Visa Token Service (VTS): Manages Visa network tokens. Mastercard MDES: Manages Mastercard tokens. Each maintains a vault mapping DPANs to real PANs. Also manage lifecycle: provisioning, suspension, deletion.

Token states

Inactive: Provisioning in progress. Active: Ready. Suspended: Temp unavailable (card lost/stolen). Deactivated: Permanently inactive.

Account Updater: When physical card expires, issuer updates PAN mapping. DPAN on your phone keeps working automatically.
Quick Check
Understanding token management
Cardholder reports card lost. What happens to Apple Pay tokens?
ANothing — tokens independent
BTokens suspended as precaution
CAuto-deleted, must re-provision
DMerchants notified to reject
Module 3 · Chapter 13 · Lesson 30

Token provisioning — the six-step process

Adding a card to a wallet: the technical handshake.

The flow

1. Enrolment: You photo card. Apple sends to TSP. 2. Lookup: TSP decrypts PAN, identifies issuer by BIN. 3. Issuer approval: TSP asks issuer via API. 4. ID&V: Issuer sends you verification text/app prompt. 5. Token generation: DPAN + cryptographic keys generated. 6. Delivery: Token stored securely on phone (SE or HCE).

SE vs HCE: Secure Element (chip) = tamper-resistant hardware. Host Card Emulation (cloud) = software. SE slightly more secure, HCE more flexible.

Module 3 · Chapter 14 · Lesson 31

Machine learning detects fraud in milliseconds

Modern fraud detection goes beyond rules to find novel patterns.

What ML adds

Rules are reactive (written after seeing fraud pattern). ML is proactive (identifies patterns not yet in rules). ML finds non-linear feature combinations.

Feature engineering

Card-level: Days since issued, historical decline rate, std dev of amounts. Behavioural: Typing speed, copy-paste detected. Network: IP reputation, cards per IP this hour. Temporal: Hour of day, transaction velocity. Graph: Device clustering, email patterns.

Production reality: Gradient boosted trees (XGBoost) dominate over neural networks. Fast inference, good tabular data handling, interpretable.
Quick Check
Understanding ML fraud
ML flags transaction high-risk, but no single rule triggered. ML advantage?
AML always more accurate
BML cheaper
CML finds non-obvious feature combinations
DML accesses secret data
Module 3 · Chapter 14 · Lesson 32

Real-time risk orchestration

Single score isn't enough. Production systems run multiple models, rules, and data sources in parallel under 300ms.

The pipeline

1. Pre-screening rules: Hard blocks (known bad IPs). 2. Feature extraction: Enrich with real-time lookups. 3. Model ensemble: Run gradient boosted trees, neural networks, rules in parallel. 4. Policy engine: Apply business rules. 5. Decisioning: Approve / Challenge / Decline. 6. Feedback loop: Chargebacks fed back into retraining.

Metric obsession: Teams track both fraud rate AND false positive rate daily. Tight threshold catches fraud but loses revenue. Loose threshold keeps revenue but lets fraud through.
Quick Check
Understanding orchestration
Team tightens ML threshold (block more). Trade-off?
AFraud improves with no downside
BMore fraud blocked, but more legitimate also declined
CModel runs faster
DInterchange rates drop
Module 3 · Chapter 15 · Lesson 33

Scheme monitoring programmes

Visa (VDMP), Mastercard (ECP) actively monitor and fine merchants exceeding fraud/chargeback thresholds.

Visa

VDMP: Chargeback ratio ≥0.65% → fines from month 4, escalate each month. VFMP: Fraud ratio ≥0.65% → fines, liability shift changes.

Mastercard

ECP: Ratio ≥1% AND count ≥100 → £1k–£100k monthly fines.

Cumulative cost: Month 4–6 in programme: £50/excess chargeback. Spend a year in VDMP = hundreds of thousands in fines. Acquirers warn months before charges become critical.

CEntry into VDMP/ECP, escalating monthly fines
DSchemes contact customers directly
Module 3 · Chapter 15 · Lesson 34

Compelling Evidence 3.0 (CE 3.0)

Visa's tool for merchants to fight friendly fraud using transaction history.

What it is

Friendly fraud = legitimate cardholders disputing real transactions. Estimated 30–40% of all chargebacks. CE 3.0 lets merchants provide evidence of prior, undisputed transactions from same cardholder on same device = proof cardholder was known before.

CE 3.0 requirements

Provide 2+ prior transactions: completed 120–365 days before dispute, not themselves disputed, same device fingerprint AND IP as disputed transaction, account created 120+ days before dispute.

Critical implication: Must collect and retain device fingerprint + IP for 13 months. Not collecting it = no CE 3.0 defence. Merchants need this data retention as compliance requirement now.
Quick Check
Understanding CE 3.0
Merchant has prior purchase history but never stored device fingerprints. Can use CE 3.0?
AYes — purchase history sufficient
BNo — device fingerprint matching is mandatory
CYes — email history can substitute
DCE 3.0 is optional
